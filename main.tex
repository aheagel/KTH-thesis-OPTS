\chapter{Introduction}
    \section{Context and Motivation}
    The modern financial services industry relies heavily on data-driven decision-making to manage credit card portfolios, assess customer segments, target audiences, and optimize campaign profitability \cite{sargeant_algorithmic_2023}. However, much of this segmentation currently depends on manually defined rules and heuristics, which are typically formulated through an analyst's extensive, hands-on experience with the data.
    
    However, this manual approach is inherently unscalable. As data volumes grow and the dimensionality of available features increases, human-driven analysis can only accommodate an ever-decreasing fraction of the true complexity within the customer base. To overcome these limitations, unsupervised machine learning offers a promising, data-driven alternative. 
    
    The objective of applying this methodology is not merely to search for hidden anomalies, but first and foremost to demonstrate that algorithmic approaches can reliably identify and group standard, expected behavioral patterns. By successfully capturing this foundational business logic as a baseline, the method can then be further leveraged to uncover complex, latent customer segments derived from highly nuanced account behavior. Ultimately, this research aims to show how hybrid techniques—combining deterministic heuristic filtering with scalable machine learning—can provide a comprehensive segmentation strategy free from rigid, predefined assumptions.
    
    \section{Problem Statement}
    Despite the availability of comprehensive transactional and profitability data, translating these raw operational databases into cohesive customer segments presents a significant engineering and analytical challenge. Financial datasets are inherently complex: they are characterized by millions of records, high dimensionality, and multiple time horizons.
    
    Furthermore, transactional columns are frequently sparse (zero-heavy), which poses a unique hurdle for unsupervised learning algorithms to process effectively. The core problem addressed in this thesis is the design and implementation of an end-to-end data processing and clustering pipeline capable of navigating this complexity. To generate meaningful insights, the raw data must be carefully aggregated, filtered, and engineered to ensure that highly correlated features do not skew the results. Ultimately, the challenge lies in building a scalable methodology that can distill raw financial data into distinct, economically interpretable customer segments.
    
    \section{Research Objectives}
    To address this problem, this thesis is guided by the following primary research objectives:
    
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Develop a Scalable Data Aggregation Pipeline:} Construct a robust methodology to merge and summarize diverse financial databases into unified annual feature profiles, including deterministic pre-segmentation to handle extreme data sparsity.
        \item \textbf{Execute Unsupervised Customer Segmentation:} Apply clustering algorithms to the engineered dataset to empirically identify distinct groups of active credit card agreements, utilizing quantitative scoring metrics to determine the optimal number of segments.
        \item \textbf{Profile Financial and Behavioral Characteristics:} Interpret the resulting clusters by analyzing their defining features, such as spending behavior across different categories.
    \end{enumerate}
    
    \section{Scope and Limitations}
    \label{sec:limitations}
    The scope of this research is defined by a targeted sample of approximately 500,000 unique credit card agreements, focusing specifically on annual aggregated data for the years 2024 and 2025.
    
    A primary limitation of this study is the inherent nature of unsupervised learning; because the data lacks ground-truth customer labels, validation relies on internal mathematical heuristics rather than predictive accuracy. Additionally, by aggregating to an annual level, seasonality and short-term behavioral shifts may be smoothed over or obscured. Finally, to comply with strict contractual obligations safeguarding proprietary company information, all dataset attributes have been deliberately obfuscated. While this necessary anonymization ensures data privacy and security, it inherently restricts access to granular details and may constrain the depth of contextual interpretation.

    \section{Customer Segmentation in Retail Banking}
    \label{sec:banking_segmentation}
    In the financial services sector, credit card portfolios represent a complex ecosystem of customer behaviors, borrowing habits, and repayment patterns. Traditionally, institutions segmented these portfolios by applying manually defined rules to frameworks like the RFM (Recency, Frequency, Monetary) model to assess risk and profitability.
    
    In a retail banking context, this framework evaluates three core dimensions of account activity: \textit{Recency} measures the time elapsed since a customer's last transaction, indicating their current engagement level; \textit{Frequency} captures the total volume of transactions within a specific period, serving as a proxy for card reliance; and \textit{Monetary} quantifies the total transaction value or outstanding balance, directly tying customer behavior to institutional profitability \cite{wilbert_recency_2023}.
    
    While this manual, rule-based approach is effective for simple datasets, it struggles to scale and capture the nuances of high-dimensional data. Consequently, modern analytical approaches do not discard these foundational metrics; instead, they integrate core RFM variables with additional behavioral and demographic features to serve as inputs for unsupervised machine learning models. Furthermore, simple heuristic frameworks remain highly effective for deterministic tasks—such as explicitly identifying and filtering out completely dormant or inactive accounts. By isolating these extremes, machine learning models can be focused entirely on algorithmically discovering latent, multivariate patterns within the active user base, rather than getting caught on simple binary boundaries \cite{yan_bank_2025}.
    
    \section{Unsupervised Machine Learning}
    \label{sec:unsupervised_ml}
    Unlike supervised machine learning, which relies on a set of labeled training data to predict a known target variable, unsupervised machine learning focuses on discovering hidden structures and intrinsic patterns within unlabeled datasets. The primary objective of an unsupervised algorithm is not to map inputs to a predefined output, but rather to infer the natural topology, relationships, or distributions inherent in the data itself \cite{naeem_unsupervised_2023}.
    
    In the context of financial customer segmentation, ground-truth labels defining distinct "customer archetypes" are rarely known in advance. While traditional approaches attempt to force customers into rigid, predefined demographic or financial buckets, unsupervised learning provides a purely data-driven methodology. By algorithmically evaluating the multivariate similarities across diverse account features, such as transaction categories, utilization rates, and repayment behaviors, unsupervised models can autonomously partition the active customer base into cohesive, economically meaningful segments without human bias.
    
        \subsection{K-means}
        \label{subsec:kmeans}
        In our study, our primary focus will be on the $K$-means clustering algorithm \cite{naeem_unsupervised_2023}. Its computational efficiency and robust theoretical foundation make it particularly well-suited for partitioning large-scale, multidimensional financial datasets.
        
        The $K$-means algorithm aims to partition a given dataset into $K$ distinct, non-overlapping clusters by minimizing the intra-cluster variance, commonly referred to as the Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS) or Inertia. Let our financial dataset be represented as an $n \times d$ matrix, where we have $n$ observations $X = \{x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n\}$ and each observation $x_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is a $d$-dimensional vector of financial features. We seek to partition the dataset into a set of $K$ clusters $S = \{S_1, S_2, \dots, S_K\}$, where $K \le n$. The formal objective is to find the assignment of data points to clusters that minimizes the following cost function:
        
        \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:kmeanscost}
            J(S) = \sum_{i=1}^{K} \sum_{x \in S_i} \|x - \mu_i\|^2
        \end{equation}
        
        where $\mu_i$ represents the geometric centroid (or mean) of the points assigned to cluster $S_i$, and $\|x - \mu_i\|^2$ denotes the squared Euclidean distance between a data point and its respective cluster centroid.
        
        Because finding the exact global optimum for this problem is NP-hard, $K$-means typically employs an iterative refinement technique known as Lloyd's algorithm. The process begins by initializing $K$ centroids, $\mu_1^{(0)}, \mu_2^{(0)}, \dots, \mu_K^{(0)}$, often chosen randomly from the dataset or via methods such as $K$-means++ to improve convergence speed and avoid poor local minima \cite{arthur_k-means_2007}. The algorithm then alternates between two core operations:
        
        \begin{enumerate}
            \item \textbf{Assignment Step:} Each data point is assigned to the cluster whose centroid yields the least squared Euclidean distance. For the $t$-th iteration, the updated cluster $S_i^{(t)}$ is defined mathematically as:
            \begin{equation}
                S_i^{(t)} = \left\{ x_p : \|x_p - \mu_i^{(t)}\|^2 \le \|x_p - \mu_j^{(t)}\|^2 \ \forall j, 1 \le j \le K \right\}
            \end{equation}
        
            \item \textbf{Update Step:} Once all data points have been assigned, the new centroids for the $(t+1)$-th iteration are recalculated as the center of mass of the points in each respective cluster:
            \begin{equation}
                \mu_i^{(t+1)} = \frac{1}{|S_i^{(t)}|} \sum_{x_j \in S_i^{(t)}} x_j
            \end{equation}
        \end{enumerate}
        
        These two steps are repeated iteratively until convergence is achieved—specifically, when the cluster assignments no longer change ($S_i^{(t)} = S_i^{(t-1)}$), or when a predefined tolerance for the reduction in WCSS is met. By mathematically minimizing the variance within each grouping, the algorithm organically isolates distinct customer archetypes defined by their shared multivariate financial behaviors.
        
        \subsection{Mini-Batch K-means}
        \label{subsec:minibatch_kmeans}
        The principles of stochastic descent provide the theoretical foundation for the Mini-Batch $K$-means algorithm (see Section \ref{sec:descent_optimization}). As established in Equation \ref{eq:kmeanscost}, the $K$-means cost function (WCSS) is inherently non-convex, meaning optimization relies on finding a stable local minimum.
        
        In standard $K$-means (Lloyd's algorithm), computing the distance between all $n$ points and $K$ centroids during the assignment step is analogous to a full batch evaluation, which scales poorly. Mini-Batch $K$-means mitigates this by applying stochastic optimization to the centroid updates \cite{sculley_web-scale_2010}. At iteration $t$, a random mini-batch $B$ of size $b$ is drawn from the dataset. Let $B_i \subset B$ represent the subset of points in the mini-batch assigned to the $i$-th centroid, $\mu_i$.
        
        Instead of replacing the centroid entirely, the algorithm performs an incremental update that shifts the centroid toward the mean of the new data points. This is fundamentally a stochastic gradient descent step on the WCSS cost function. Crucially, the algorithm maintains a per-center count, $v_{i, t}$, which tracks the cumulative number of data points assigned to centroid $\mu_i$ up to iteration $t$. The centroid is updated as follows:
        
        \begin{equation}
            \mu_i^{(t+1)} = \mu_i^{(t)} + \frac{1}{v_{i, t}} \sum_{x \in B_i} \left( x - \mu_i^{(t)} \right)
        \end{equation}
        
        This update mechanism elegantly reveals the underlying learning rate of the algorithm: $\alpha_t = \frac{1}{v_{i, t}}$. Because the cumulative point count $v_{i, t}$ increases linearly with the number of iterations, the learning rate decays proportionally to $1/t$. This specific decay rate perfectly satisfies the Robbins-Monro convergence criteria, and thus Mini-Batch $K$-means is theoretically guaranteed to converge to a stable local optimum. By framing the centroid shifts as stochastic gradient updates with a naturally decaying learning rate, the algorithm achieves drastic reductions in computational complexity without sacrificing theoretical convergence guarantees.
        
        \subsection{K-median}
        \label{subsec:kmedian}
        While the $K$-means algorithm is computationally efficient, its reliance on squared Euclidean distances makes it inherently sensitive to outliers. In financial datasets, where extreme values are common, these outliers can disproportionately skew the cluster centroids.
        
        To address this vulnerability, the $K$-median clustering algorithm serves as a robust alternative. Instead of minimizing the variance, $K$-median aims to partition the dataset by minimizing the absolute deviations between data points and their cluster centers. Using the same dataset matrix $X$ and cluster set $S$ defined previously, the formal objective of $K$-median is to find the assignment that minimizes the following cost function:
        
        \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:kmediancost}
            J_{median}(S) = \sum_{i=1}^{K} \sum_{x \in S_i} \|x - m_i\|_1
        \end{equation}
        
        where $m_i$ represents the median vector of the points assigned to cluster $S_i$, and $\|x - m_i\|_1$ denotes the $L_1$ norm, commonly known as the Manhattan distance, between a data point and its cluster median.
        
        Similar to $K$-means, the optimization of this cost function is achieved through an iterative heuristic framework. After initializing $K$ medians, the algorithm alternates between assigning points based on the smallest $L_1$ distance and updating the medians independently for each feature dimension. By substituting the mean with the median and the squared $L_2$ norm with the $L_1$ norm, the $K$-median algorithm effectively prevents extreme financial anomalies from dominating the spatial definition of the clusters, resulting in more representative and economically stable customer archetypes.
    
    \section{Cluster Evaluation Metrics}
    \label{sec:evaluation_metrics}
    Determining the optimal number of clusters, $K$, in unsupervised learning requires quantitative evaluation metrics, as ground-truth labels are unavailable. This study utilizes two primary metrics: Inertia and the Silhouette Score.
    
        \subsection{Inertia (Within-Cluster Sum of Squares)}
        As defined in Equation \ref{eq:kmeanscost}, Inertia measures the total intra-cluster variance. While minimizing Inertia is the core objective of $K$-means, this value naturally decreases as $K$ increases, eventually reaching zero if every data point becomes its own individual cluster. Therefore, Inertia is typically evaluated using the "elbow method", where the optimal $K$ is identified at the inflection point where the rate of variance reduction sharply diminishes.
        
        \subsection{Silhouette Score}
        To evaluate both cluster cohesion (how tightly grouped the points in a cluster are) and cluster separation (how distinct the clusters are from one another), the Silhouette Score is employed. For a given data point $i$, the silhouette coefficient $s(i)$ is defined mathematically as:
        
        \begin{equation}
            s(i) = \frac{b(i) - a(i)}{\max(a(i), b(i))}
        \end{equation}
        
        where $a(i)$ is the mean distance between point $i$ and all other points assigned to the same cluster, and $b(i)$ is the minimum mean distance between point $i$ and all points in any other cluster (i.e., the nearest neighboring cluster). 
        
        The silhouette score ranges from $-1$ to $1$. A value close to $1$ indicates that the data point is perfectly matched to its own cluster and cleanly separated from neighboring clusters. A value near $0$ suggests the point is on or very close to the decision boundary between two clusters, and negative values indicate that the point may have been assigned to the wrong cluster. The global Silhouette Score of a model is calculated as the mean $s(i)$ across all data points, providing a straightforward and robust metric to mathematically determine the optimal partition count $K$.
    
    \section{Dimensionality Reduction}
    \label{sec:dimensionality_reduction}
    
        \subsection{Principal Component Analysis (PCA)}
        \label{subsec:pca}
        While partitioning algorithms like $K$-means group observations into distinct clusters, financial datasets are frequently characterized by high dimensionality. As the number of features $d$ increases, the concept of distance becomes inherently less discriminative—a mathematical phenomenon widely known as the curse of dimensionality.
        
        To mitigate this, Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is often employed as a preliminary unsupervised learning step to reduce the feature space while preserving the maximum possible variance of the original dataset \cite{naeem_unsupervised_2023}. PCA seeks an orthogonal linear transformation that projects the data into a new $k$-dimensional subspace, where $k < d$, maximizing the variance of the projected data.
        
        The spatial relationships between the features are captured by the $d \times d$ sample covariance matrix. By solving the algebraic eigenvalue problem for the covariance matrix, we identify eigenvectors (dictating the direction of the principal axes) and eigenvalues (quantifying the magnitude of the dataset's variance explained along that specific axis). The first $k$ eigenvectors are concatenated to form a projection matrix, mapping the high-dimensional dataset into a reduced subspace.
        
        In the context of financial customer segmentation, applying PCA prior to clustering effectively filters out stochastic noise and redundant, collinear variables. This dimensionality reduction can accelerate optimization and stabilize cluster assignments by grounding distance calculations exclusively in the most statistically significant dimensions.
        
        \subsection{Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP)}
        \label{subsec:umap}
        While PCA provides an efficient mechanism for capturing the global variance and linear relationships within a dataset, it inherently struggles to resolve complex, non-linear patterns. In high-dimensional financial datasets, data points frequently lie on complex manifolds that linear projections simply flatten.
        
        To address this limitation, non-linear dimensionality reduction techniques like Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) \cite{mcinnes_umap_2020} are often considered. UMAP is a modern manifold learning technique grounded in Riemannian geometry and algebraic topology. Unlike PCA, UMAP operates by constructing a high-dimensional fuzzy topological representation of the data and subsequently optimizing a low-dimensional graph to be as structurally similar as possible. This optimization is achieved by minimizing the fuzzy set cross-entropy $C$ between the high-dimensional and low-dimensional graph weights.
        
        While theoretically robust for capturing non-linear topologies and maintaining local proximities of customer profiles, constructing the requisite high-dimensional fuzzy graphs is highly computationally intensive. For massive financial datasets containing millions of observations, UMAP often becomes computationally infeasible without aggressive down-sampling, limiting its utility as a primary reduction step in full-population pipelines.
    
    \section{Iterative Descent Optimization}
    \label{sec:descent_optimization}
    To understand the mechanics of large-scale clustering, it is necessary to first establish the general principles of iterative descent optimization. In applied machine learning, optimization problems frequently require minimizing a differentiable scalar cost function. When analytical solutions are computationally intractable, iterative methods such as Gradient Descent are employed to update parameters by taking steps in the direction of the negative gradient.
    
    Evaluating the full gradient at every iteration is computationally prohibitive for massive datasets ($n \gg 1$). Stochastic gradient methods bypass this bottleneck by approximating the true gradient using a small, randomly sampled subset of the data known as a mini-batch. Because the mini-batch gradient is an unbiased estimator of the true gradient, the algorithm descends the cost surface in expectation. To theoretically guarantee convergence in the presence of stochastic noise, the learning rate must strictly satisfy the Robbins-Monro conditions for stochastic approximation \cite{robbins_stochastic_1951}.
    
    \section{Data Scaling and Normalization}
    \label{sec:data_scaling}
    Distance-based clustering algorithms, such as $K$-means and $K$-median, as well as variance-maximizing techniques like PCA, are mathematically sensitive to the scale of the input features. In credit card portfolios, financial variables inherently span vastly different magnitudes (e.g., transaction counts from 0 to 50 versus monetary spend from \$0 to \$50,000). To ensure all variables contribute equally to the multidimensional topology, data scaling is a mandatory preprocessing step.
    
        \subsection{Z-Score Standardization}
        \label{subsec:zscore_scaling}
        A standard approach is Z-score standardization, which rescales each feature column to have a mean of zero and a standard deviation of one \cite{pinheiro_impact_2025}. While effective for roughly normally distributed data, it can still be skewed by extreme outliers.
        
        \subsection{Min-Max Scaling}
        \label{subsec:minmax_scaling}
        When the objective is to bound the data within a strict, predefined range—typically $[0, 1]$—Min-Max scaling is utilized. This transformation preserves the exact geometric shape of the original distribution but forces all features onto the same finite scale \cite{pinheiro_impact_2025}. Because it relies strictly on global maximum and minimum values, it is highly vulnerable to anomalies.
        
        \subsection{Robust Scaling}
        \label{subsec:robust_scaling}
        Given that financial datasets are notoriously heavy-tailed, Robust Scaling often provides a more stable transformation \cite{pinheiro_impact_2025}. Instead of utilizing the mean and standard deviation, this method centers the data using the median ($Q_2$) and scales it according to the Interquartile Range (IQR). By utilizing non-parametric statistics, the robust scaler ensures that the bulk of the customer data remains proportionate, preventing financial anomalies from distorting the geometry of the clustering space.
    
    \section{Feature Engineering and Selection}
    \label{sec:feature_engineering_selection}
    Preparing high-dimensional empirical datasets for unsupervised machine learning requires rigorous preprocessing. Observational data is frequently intrinsically noisy, highly skewed, and plagued by redundant variables.
    
        \subsection{Logarithmic Transformation}
        \label{subsec:log_transform}
        A common characteristic of many continuous empirical features is a severe right-skewness. For distance-metric algorithms, these extreme right-tail outliers exert a disproportionate gravitational pull. To mitigate this, shifted logarithmic transformations ($x' = \log(x + c)$) are frequently applied to compress the long tail and approximate a more normal distribution, mapping strict zero values strictly back to zero.
        
        \subsection{Near-Zero Variance Filtering}
        \label{subsec:nzv_filtering}
        High-dimensional datasets frequently contain highly specialized or niche variables, resulting in a significantly sparse feature matrix. Features that exhibit near-zero variance contribute negligible discriminatory power to the clustering process. To address this, near-zero variance filtering is employed as a primary feature selection step, algorithmically pruning features that fail to meet a predefined minimum variance threshold.
        
        \subsection{Correlation Thresholding}
        \label{subsec:correlation_thresholding}
        Beyond sparsity, datasets frequently exhibit severe multicollinearity. In a Euclidean space, two perfectly correlated features will mathematically exert twice the weight on the distance calculation as a single, independent feature. To neutralize this structural bias, correlation thresholding is applied to prune redundant variables using the Pearson correlation coefficient $\rho$. An absolute correlation threshold is established—in this research, bounded at a strict 0.90 magnitude. When a pair of features exceeds this threshold, one is removed, ensuring an orthogonal, balanced feature space \cite{mangal_comparative_2018}.

\chapter{Methodology}
\label{chap:Methodology}

This chapter outlines the data pipeline and machine learning workflow developed for this thesis. First, the end-to-end mathematical pipeline is detailed, from initial data acquisition through preprocessing, feature engineering, and the final application of unsupervised clustering algorithms. Finally, the chapter concludes by outlining the specific experimental cases designed to test this pipeline under different operational scenarios.

    \section{Data Acquisition and Structure}
    \label{sec:data_acquisition}
    The initial dataset consists of annual-level data containing approximately one million observations. Each observation represents a specific customer agreement at a given point in time, characterized by over 580 distinct features. To maintain a consistent unit of analysis, individual rows are tracked and treated as independent observations using a multi-index comprising \texttt{Agreement\_ID} and \texttt{Year}. This structure allows for the tracking of account behavior over multiple periods while treating each annual snapshot as a distinct data point in the feature space.
    
    \section{Data Preprocessing and Heuristic Exclusions}
    \label{sec:preprocessing}
    Before applying any machine learning algorithms, the raw dataset required several preprocessing steps to ensure data quality and relevance to the target customer population.
    
        \subsection{Data Cleansing}
        The initial cleansing phase focused on standardizing the dataset and removing artifacts generated during the data export process. Analysis of the missing data revealed that rows containing null values (\texttt{NaN}) constituted less than 1\% of the total observations. Given this negligible proportion, listwise deletion was employed to drop these incomplete records. This approach was selected over imputation to prevent the introduction of synthetic bias, ensuring that all downstream distance calculations were based strictly on empirical observations. Furthermore, rows exhibiting fundamental structural errors—such as logically inconsistent temporal timestamps or corrupted feature mappings—were entirely removed from the analytical pipeline.
        
        \subsection{Rule-Based Segmentation (Pre-Clustering)}
        An optional exploratory step built into the pipeline is a rule-based segmentation approach. The rationale is to explicitly isolate specific, predefined customer cohorts prior to the application of unsupervised models. Two primary groups of interest were established for this pre-segmentation phase: Group Z and Group C. These groups represent distinct behavioral or operational extremes within the portfolio based on specific internal business logic. By deterministically assigning these accounts to predefined segments beforehand, the primary clustering algorithms are freed from grouping obvious structural extremes, allowing them to focus entirely on parsing the nuanced variance within the active majority of the portfolio.
    
    \section{Feature Engineering and Transformation}
    \label{sec:feature_engineering}
    To manage dimensionality and ensure the models train on high-signal data, a standardized sequence of feature transformations and algorithmic filtration is applied to the dataset.
    
        \subsection{Logarithmic Transformation}
        An experimental logarithmic transformation (\texttt{log1p}) is investigated as an optional preprocessing step to handle the extreme positive skewness typical of financial data. When enabled, this transformation is exclusively applied to aggregated transactional sum data, leaving other metric types (e.g., counts, limits, percentages) in their original scale. Crucially, because this logarithmic transformation fundamentally alters the variance and the linear relationships between variables, its application directly impacts all subsequent filtration steps. Consequently, toggling this transformation dynamically changes the final feature space across all tested models.
        
        \subsection{Algorithmic Filtration}
        Following the transformation phase, automated data-agnostic filtration is applied to reduce computational bloat. First, a Near-Zero Variance (NZV) filter with a strict threshold of $10^{-4}$ is implemented to discard non-informative, largely static features. Subsequently, applying the correlation bounds discussed in Chapter \ref{chap:Background}, a filter is applied to mitigate multicollinearity, dropping variables with a Pearson correlation coefficient exceeding 0.90. This ensures the retention of only the most distinct, non-redundant behavioral proxies.
    
    \section{Scaling and Domain-Specific Weighting}
    \label{sec:scaling}
    Because distance-based clustering algorithms are highly sensitive to the magnitude of the input variables, rigorous scaling methodologies were required.
    
        \subsection{Normalization Techniques}
        Before applying global scalers, manual pre-normalization is applied to naturally bounded features. For example, utilization rates are explicitly scaled by dividing by 100 to map them to a strict continuous range without distorting their original proportional meaning. Following this, three distinct global scaling techniques are evaluated across the pipeline: Min-Max Scaler, Robust Scaler, and Standard Scaler. Testing multiple scalers ensures that the final clusters are driven by genuine behavioral patterns rather than artifacts introduced by forced normal distributions or outlier sensitivities.
        
        \subsection{Feature Weighting}
        While explicit feature weighting was considered to balance the influence of distinct financial categories, it introduces significant complexity when implemented dynamically at scale. Consequently, targeted feature weighting is treated as an optional parameter, specifically utilized within the domain-driven experimental configurations (the Normal and Improved cases). In these cases, five core financial domains were identified as holding equal theoretical importance: incoming payments, interest capitalization, credit limits, utilization rates, and financial/cash-like transactions. 
        
        However, because the algorithmic filtration processes (such as the correlation and Near-Zero Variance filters) prune the number of aggregated sub-features unevenly across these categories, standard scaling would disproportionately favor domains that retained a higher volume of sub-features. To rectify this, a custom weighting function is applied immediately prior to dimensionality reduction (PCA). The total feature weight is distributed such that each of the five core domains contributes exactly 20\% of the aggregate weight. For a given domain $d$ with $n_d$ surviving features, each individual feature within that domain is multiplied by the factor $\frac{1}{5 \cdot n_d}$. This strict normalization ensures the unsupervised models evaluate each core financial behavior equally, regardless of its underlying dimensional footprint post-filtration.
    
    \section{Dimensionality Reduction}
    \label{sec:dimensionality_reduction}
    Given the scale of the dataset, dimensionality reduction techniques were evaluated to compress the feature space and improve algorithmic efficiency prior to spatial clustering.
    
        \subsection{Principal Component Analysis (PCA)}
        Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is incorporated as an optional experimental variable. The methodology systematically investigates and compares clustering outcomes both with and without PCA to objectively assess its necessity. When enabled, PCA dynamically projects the features into a lower-dimensional space while retaining a target cumulative variance, attempting to mitigate the curse of dimensionality.
        
        \subsection{Alternative Methods: Manifold Learning}
        Alternative non-linear dimensionality reduction techniques were also considered, specifically Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP). However, preliminary testing revealed that applying UMAP to a dense dataset of approximately one million observations was computationally infeasible without aggressive down-sampling. To maintain the structural integrity of the full population analysis, UMAP is excluded from the primary quantitative methodology.
    
    \section{Clustering Algorithms}
    \label{sec:clustering_algorithms}
    The core of the machine learning workflow relies on distance-based, unsupervised partitioning algorithms to identify latent customer archetypes.
    
        \subsection{Model Selection}
        The primary algorithms evaluated are K-Means and K-Medians. Standard K-Means is tested for its efficiency in variance minimization and its widespread baseline acceptance. Conversely, K-Medians is evaluated to explicitly address the extreme outliers and heavy-tailed distributions inherent in financial transaction data; by minimizing the Manhattan distance ($L_1$ norm) rather than the squared Euclidean distance ($L_2$ norm), it offers a theoretically more robust partitioning of skewed variables. Additionally, the computational viability of Mini-Batch K-Means is briefly assessed as a highly scalable alternative.
        
        \subsection{Hyperparameter Tuning}
        Determining the optimal number of customer segments, or clusters ($K$), is critical. Across all testing phases, the value of $K$ is tuned by evaluating a range of cluster counts against two primary objective metrics: Inertia (the within-cluster sum of squares) to identify structural "elbow" points, and the Silhouette Score to quantify global cluster cohesion and separation. Most of the initial results focused mainly on the Silhouette Score as it provided a simple yet straightforward metric to determine an optimal $K$. In this particular case calclculating the silhouetta score over all data points is infeasible, thus a sample size of 50000 was arbitrary set.
    
    \section{Cluster Profiling and Interpretation}
    \label{sec:cluster_profiling}
    Following algorithmic partitioning, the resulting mathematical clusters must be translated back into actionable financial insights.
    
        \subsection{Evaluation in Original Feature Space}
        Because distance algorithms require normalized data to function, the raw outputs lack immediate business context. To resolve this, all resulting cluster assignments are mapped directly back to the original, unscaled dataset. This ensures that the final customer archetypes are profiled using actual monetary transaction sums, genuine utilization percentages, and exact count metrics.
        
        \subsection{Visualization and Temporal Dynamics}
        To profile the segments, comparative statistical visualizations—such as customized boxplots and median-centric comparative analyses—are utilized to isolate defining financial characteristics. Finally, the methodology concludes by examining temporal dynamics to quantify customer segment transitions. This analysis is restricted to accounts possessing multiple annual observations (specifically, two observations, given the delimited 2024 to 2025 timeframe). By tracking the assigned cluster label for each unique \texttt{Agreement\_ID} across both periods, a temporal transition matrix is constructed using a cross-tabulation of the initial and subsequent cluster assignments. These raw transition counts are then row-normalized to yield empirical transition probabilities, detailing the exact percentage of accounts that either migrated to a new behavioral archetype or remained stable within their original cluster over the observed period.
    
    \section{Experimental Design: Pipeline Configurations}
    \label{sec:experimental_design}
    Having established the technical pipeline, the methodology was deployed across three distinct experimental cases to rigorously evaluate the impact of feature selection and data preprocessing on the resulting customer archetypes.
    
        \subsection{Case 1: The Naive Baseline (Data-Agnostic)}
        The Naive case simulates a purely algorithmic approach, relying on automated dimensionality reduction rather than financial domain knowledge. An initial, highly redundant feature space of 408 continuous variables was generated using exhaustive temporal aggregations across all available base metrics. Applying the standardized algorithmic filtration (NZV and correlation limits), this automated reduction left the Naive model with an uncurated space of 220 features or if logged 157 features. As noted in Section~\ref{sec:feature_engineering}, this final feature count fluctuates depending on whether the optional logarithmic transformation is applied, as it alters which variables survive filtration. Crucially, in this baseline case, the massive sub-population of completely inactive accounts is retained, challenging the algorithms to handle the zero-heavy distributions natively.
        
        \subsection{Case 2: The Normal Case (Domain-Driven Selection)}
        Recognizing the potential noise of a purely data-agnostic approach, the methodology was pivoted toward business-logic-driven feature selection. The feature space was drastically scaled down by isolating core financial behaviors, specifically utilization rates and specific incoming payment/transaction categories. Applying standard aggregations exclusively to these metrics reduced the initial dataset to 29 features. A secondary application of the NZV and correlation filters further refined this space down to roughly 19 distinct variables or 16 if logged. Like the Naive baseline, the Normal case implemented no heuristic pre-clustering.
        
        \subsection{Case 3: The Improved Case (Optimized Pre-segmentation)}
        A significant structural challenge within the raw financial data is the substantial presence of inactive accounts, which act as a mathematical sink and cause clustering algorithms to become trapped in suboptimal local minima. The Improved case represents the fully optimized pipeline. It utilizes the same tightly curated, domain-driven feature space established in the Normal case. However, it activates the heuristic pre-segmentation step (detailed in Section~\ref{sec:preprocessing}) to deterministically filter out inactive accounts \textit{before} applying the unsupervised models. Inactive accounts were explicitly defined and isolated as profiles exhibiting exactly zero user-initiated transactions. By partitioning these accounts beforehand, the subsequent models are forced to focus entirely on the variance within active financial behaviors.

\section{Evaluating Scaling Methodologies: The Superiority of Min-Max}
\label{sec:results_minmax}
Before finalizing the pipeline architecture, standard scaling techniques were comparatively evaluated to determine their impact on cluster quality. This evaluation revealed that the Min-Max scaler consistently yielded superior results across the board compared to traditional Z-score standardization or Robust scaling. 

Because financial datasets are heavily bound by absolute zeroes (e.g., zero balance, zero transactions), forcing the variables into a strict $[0, 1]$ interval via Min-Max scaling preserved the intrinsic geometric constraints of the data without artificially shifting the mean. When evaluating the Naive case, the Min-Max scaler successfully stabilized the variance generated by the 157 uncurated features, pulling extreme outliers into a finite space and preventing them from infinitely skewing the $L_2$ norm distances. Similarly, in the curated Normal cases, applying the Min-Max scaler significantly boosted spatial cohesion, allowing distance-based algorithms to effectively weigh each behavioral pillar equally without being dominated by the unbounded magnitude of sheer transaction sums.

\subsection{K-Means vs. K-Medians Clustering Performance}

However, within the Improved configuration—where the feature space is tightly curated to 16 variables and operational extremes are heuristically removed—$K$-medians demonstrates significant analytical potential and vastly improved execution speeds. 

For the $K$-means model (optimizing the squared $L_2$ norm), evaluation across the predefined parameter grid revealed an optimal partition at $K=4$, achieving a maximum Silhouette score of 0.3870. The algorithm demonstrated high computational efficiency with a convergence time of 0.60 seconds. 

In stark contrast to variance minimization, the $K$-medians algorithm (optimizing the $L_1$ norm or Manhattan distance) yielded a completely different geometric outcome. Providing theoretical robustness against the remaining extreme financial outliers, $K$-medians favored a highly stable binary partition at $K=2$, yielding a superior Silhouette score of 0.4509.
