\chapter{Introduction}
    \section{Context and motivation}
    The modern financial services industry relies heavily on data-driven decision-making to manage credit card portfolios, assess customer segments, target audiences, and optimize campaign profitability \cite{sargeant_algorithmic_2023}.
    However, much of this segmentation currently depends on manually defined rules and heuristics, which are typically formulated through an analyst's extensive, hands-on experience with the data.
    However, this manual approach is inherently unscalable. As data volumes grow and the dimensionality of available features increases, human-driven analysis can only accommodate an ever-decreasing fraction of the true complexity within the customer base.
    To overcome these limitations, unsupervised machine learning offers a promising, data-driven alternative.
    The objective of applying this methodology is not merely to search for hidden anomalies, but first and foremost to demonstrate that algorithmic approaches can reliably identify and group standard, expected behavioral patterns.
    By successfully capturing this foundational business logic as a baseline, the method can then be further leveraged to uncover complex, latent customer segments derived from highly nuanced account behavior.
    Ultimately, this research aims to show how hybrid techniques, combining deterministic heuristic filtering with scalable machine learning, can provide a comprehensive segmentation strategy free from rigid, predefined assumptions.
    
    \section{Problem statement}
    Despite the availability of comprehensive transactional and profitability data, translating these raw operational databases into cohesive customer segments presents a significant engineering and analytical challenge.
    Financial datasets are inherently complex. They are characterized by millions of records, high dimensionality, and multiple time horizons.
    Furthermore, transactional columns are frequently sparse and zero-heavy, which poses a unique hurdle for unsupervised learning algorithms to process effectively.
    The core problem addressed in this thesis is the design and implementation of an end-to-end data processing and clustering pipeline capable of navigating this complexity.
    To generate meaningful insights, the raw data must be carefully aggregated, filtered, and engineered to ensure that highly correlated features do not skew the results.
    Ultimately, the challenge lies in building a scalable methodology that can distill raw financial data into distinct, economically interpretable customer segments.
    
    \section{Research objectives}
    To address this problem, this thesis is guided by the following primary research objectives:
    
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Develop a scalable data aggregation pipeline:} Construct a robust methodology to merge and summarize diverse financial databases into unified annual feature profiles, including deterministic pre-segmentation to handle extreme data sparsity.
        \item \textbf{Execute unsupervised customer segmentation:} Apply clustering algorithms to the engineered dataset to empirically identify distinct groups of active credit card agreements, utilizing quantitative scoring metrics to determine the optimal number of segments.
        \item \textbf{Profile financial and behavioral characteristics:} Interpret the resulting clusters by analyzing their defining features, such as spending behavior across different categories.
    \end{enumerate}
    
    \section{Scope and limitations}
    \label{sec:limitations}
    The scope of this research is defined by a targeted sample of approximately 500,000 unique credit card agreements across the years 2024 and 2025, yielding a longitudinal dataset of 999,128 annual observations.
    A primary limitation of this study is the inherent nature of unsupervised learning;
    because the data lacks ground-truth customer labels, validation relies on internal mathematical heuristics rather than predictive accuracy \cite{naeem_unsupervised_2023}.
    Additionally, by aggregating to an annual level, seasonality and short-term behavioral shifts may be smoothed over or obscured.
    Finally, to comply with strict contractual obligations safeguarding proprietary company information, all dataset attributes have been deliberately obfuscated.
    Beyond data-specific constraints, the algorithmic scope of this thesis was deliberately restricted to partitioning models characterized by strong global consensus and linear explainability, such as $K$-means and $K$-medians.
    Given that this research represents a primary, exploratory entry into automated portfolio analysis, establishing a transparent, highly interpretable baseline was prioritized over the deployment of complex, non-linear architectures.
    In a retail banking ecosystem, final customer segmentations must ultimately be delivered to corporate boards and non-technical stakeholders who require explicit, deterministic rules to authorize capital allocation and product campaigns.
    Highly complex model topologies often yield probabilistic or arbitrary cluster boundaries that obscure business intuition.
    Furthermore, prioritizing geometrically straightforward models allowed for a more wise allocation of the computational budget.
    Rather than consuming resource overhead on the iterative estimation of highly sensitive, multi-parameter probability densities, computational power was strategically redirected toward executing extensive hyperparameter grid searches, robust domain-specific feature weighting, and alternative scaling evaluations across the entire active dataset.

\chapter{Background}
\label{chap:Background}
This chapter establishes the theoretical foundation for the methodologies applied in this research.
It first explores the domain of credit card portfolio management and standard financial metrics.
Subsequently, it details the mathematical principles behind unsupervised machine learning, specific dimensionality reduction techniques, clustering algorithms, and the quantitative metrics used to evaluate them.
    
    \section{Customer segmentation in retail banking}
    \label{sec:banking_segmentation}
    In the financial services sector, credit card portfolios represent a complex ecosystem of customer behaviors, borrowing habits, and repayment patterns.
    Traditionally, institutions segmented these portfolios by applying manually defined rules to frameworks like the RFM model to assess risk and profitability.
    In a retail banking context, this framework evaluates three core dimensions of account activity.
    \textit{Recency} measures the time elapsed since a customer's last transaction, indicating their current engagement level.
    \textit{Frequency} captures the total volume of transactions within a specific period, serving as a proxy for card reliance.
    \textit{Monetary} quantifies the total transaction value or outstanding balance, directly tying customer behavior to institutional profitability \cite{wilbert_recency_2023}.
    While this manual, rule-based approach is effective for simple datasets, it struggles to scale and capture the nuances of high-dimensional data.
    Consequently, modern analytical approaches do not discard these foundational metrics;
    instead, they integrate core RFM variables with additional behavioral and demographic features to serve as inputs for unsupervised machine learning models.
    Furthermore, simple heuristic frameworks remain highly effective for deterministic tasks, such as explicitly identifying and filtering out completely dormant or inactive accounts.
    By isolating these extremes, machine learning models can be focused entirely on algorithmically discovering latent, multivariate patterns within the active user base, rather than getting caught on simple binary boundaries \cite{yan_bank_2025}.
    
    \section{Unsupervised machine learning}
    \label{sec:unsupervised_ml}
    Unlike supervised machine learning, which relies on a set of labeled training data to predict a known target variable, unsupervised machine learning focuses on discovering hidden structures and intrinsic patterns within unlabeled datasets.
    The primary objective of an unsupervised algorithm is not to map inputs to a predefined output, but rather to infer the natural topology, relationships, or distributions inherent in the data itself \cite{naeem_unsupervised_2023}.
    In the context of financial customer segmentation, ground-truth labels defining distinct customer archetypes are rarely known in advance.
    While traditional approaches attempt to force customers into rigid, predefined demographic or financial buckets, unsupervised learning provides a purely data-driven methodology.
    By algorithmically evaluating the multivariate similarities across diverse account features, such as transaction categories, utilization rates, and repayment behaviors, unsupervised models can autonomously partition the active customer base into cohesive, economically meaningful segments without human bias.
    
        \subsection{$K$-means}
        \label{subsec:kmeans}
        In this study, the primary focus will be on the $K$-means clustering algorithm \cite{naeem_unsupervised_2023}.
        Its computational efficiency and robust theoretical foundation make it particularly well-suited for partitioning large-scale, multidimensional financial datasets.
        The $K$-means algorithm aims to partition a given dataset into $K$ distinct, non-overlapping clusters by minimizing the intra-cluster variance, commonly referred to as the Within-Cluster Sum of Squares, or WCSS.
        Let the financial dataset be represented as an $n \times d$ matrix, consisting of $n$ observations $X = \{x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n\}$ where each observation $x_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is a $d$-dimensional vector of financial features.
        The objective is to partition the dataset into a set of $K$ distinct clusters $S = \{S_1, S_2, \dots, S_K\}$, where $K \le n$.
        Formally, the task requires identifying an assignment of data points to clusters that minimizes the following cost function:
            
        \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:kmeanscost}
            J(S) = \sum_{i=1}^{K} \sum_{x \in S_i} \|x - \mu_i\|^2
        \end{equation}
        
        where $\mu_i$ represents the geometric centroid, or mean, of the points assigned to cluster $S_i$, and $\|x - \mu_i\|^2$ denotes the squared Euclidean distance between a data point and its respective cluster centroid.
        Because finding the exact global optimum for this cost function is NP-hard, standard implementations of $K$-means rely on an iterative refinement technique known as Lloyd's algorithm.
        This standard batch approach alternates between two core operations after initializing $K$ centroids, $\mu_1^{(0)}, \mu_2^{(0)}, \dots, \mu_K^{(0)}$ (frequently utilizing the $K$-means++ initialization scheme to improve convergence speed and avoid poor local minima \cite{arthur_K-means_2007}):
        
        \begin{enumerate}
            \item \textbf{Assignment step:} Each data point across the entire dataset is mapped to the cluster whose centroid yields the minimum squared Euclidean distance.
            For the $t$-th iteration, the updated cluster $S_i^{(t)}$ is defined mathematically as:
            \begin{equation}
                S_i^{(t)} = \left\{ x_p : \|x_p - \mu_i^{(t)}\|^2 \le \|x_p - \mu_j^{(t)}\|^2 \ \forall j, 1 \le j \le K \right\}
            \end{equation}
        
            \item \textbf{Update step:} Once the global assignment phase is complete, the new centroids for the $(t+1)$-th iteration are recalculated as the center of mass of all points in each respective cluster:
            \begin{equation}
                \mu_i^{(t+1)} = \frac{1}{|S_i^{(t)}|} \sum_{x_j \in S_i^{(t)}} x_j
            \end{equation}
        \end{enumerate}
        
        These two steps are repeated iteratively over the full batch of data until convergence is achieved. Convergence occurs specifically when the global cluster assignments stabilize, denoted by $S_i^{(t)} = S_i^{(t-1)}$, or when a predefined tolerance for the reduction in WCSS is met.
        By mathematically minimizing the variance within each grouping using full-population updates, this standard Lloyd framework organically isolates distinct customer archetypes defined by their shared multivariate financial behaviors.
        
        \subsection{Mini-batch $K$-means}
        \label{subsec:minibatch_kmeans}
        The principles of stochastic descent provide the theoretical foundation for the mini-batch $K$-means algorithm, which is further detailed in Section~\ref{sec:descent_optimization}.
        As established in Equation~\ref{eq:kmeanscost}, the $K$-means cost function is inherently non-convex, meaning optimization relies on finding a stable local minimum.
        In standard $K$-means using Lloyd's algorithm, computing the distance between all $n$ points and $K$ centroids during the assignment step is analogous to a full batch evaluation, which scales poorly.
        Mini-batch $K$-means mitigates this by applying stochastic optimization to the centroid updates \cite{sculley_web-scale_2010}.
        At iteration $t$, a random mini-batch $B$ of size $b$ is drawn from the dataset.
        Let $B_i \subset B$ represent the subset of points in the mini-batch assigned to the $i$-th centroid, $\mu_i$.
        Instead of replacing the centroid entirely, the algorithm performs an incremental update that shifts the centroid toward the mean of the new data points.
        This is fundamentally a stochastic gradient descent step on the WCSS cost function.
        Crucially, the algorithm maintains a per-center count, $v_{i,t}$, which tracks the cumulative number of data points assigned to centroid $\mu_i$ up to iteration $t$.
        The centroid is updated as follows:
        
        \begin{equation}
            \mu_i^{(t+1)} = \mu_i^{(t)} + \frac{1}{v_{i, t}} \sum_{x \in B_i} \left( x - \mu_i^{(t)} \right)
        \end{equation}
        
        This update mechanism elegantly reveals the underlying learning rate of the algorithm, defined as $\alpha_t = \frac{1}{v_{i, t}}$.
        Because the cumulative point count $v_{i, t}$ increases linearly with the number of iterations, the learning rate decays proportionally to $1/t$.
        This specific decay rate perfectly satisfies the Robbins-Monro convergence criteria, and thus mini-batch $K$-means is theoretically guaranteed to converge to a stable local optimum.
        By framing the centroid shifts as stochastic gradient updates with a naturally decaying learning rate, the algorithm achieves drastic reductions in computational complexity without sacrificing theoretical convergence guarantees.
        
        \subsection{$K$-medians}
        \label{subsec:kmedians}
        While the $K$-means algorithm is computationally efficient, its reliance on squared Euclidean distances makes it inherently sensitive to outliers.
        In financial datasets, where extreme values are common, these outliers can disproportionately skew the cluster centroids.
        To address this vulnerability, the $K$-medians clustering algorithm serves as a robust alternative.
        Instead of minimizing the variance, $K$-medians aims to partition the dataset by minimizing the absolute deviations between data points and their cluster centers.
        Using the same dataset matrix $X$ and cluster set $S$ defined previously, the formal objective of $K$-medians is to find the assignment that minimizes the following cost function:
        
        \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:kmedianscost}
            J_{median}(S) = \sum_{i=1}^{K} \sum_{x \in S_i} \|x - m_i\|_1
        \end{equation}
        
        where $m_i$ represents the median vector of the points assigned to cluster $S_i$, and $\|x - m_i\|_1$ denotes the $L_1$ norm, commonly known as the Manhattan distance, between a data point and its cluster median.
        Similar to $K$-means, the optimization of this cost function is achieved through an iterative heuristic framework.
        After initializing $K$ medians, the algorithm alternates between assigning points based on the smallest $L_1$ distance and updating the medians independently for each feature dimension.
        By substituting the mean with the median and the squared $L_2$ norm with the $L_1$ norm, the $K$-medians algorithm effectively prevents extreme financial anomalies from dominating the spatial definition of the clusters, resulting in more representative and economically stable customer archetypes.
        
    \section{Cluster evaluation metrics}
    \label{sec:evaluation_metrics}
    Determining the optimal number of clusters, $K$, in unsupervised learning requires quantitative evaluation metrics, as ground-truth labels are unavailable.
    This study utilizes two primary metrics: inertia and the silhouette index.
    
        \subsection{Inertia}
        As defined in Equation~\ref{eq:kmeanscost}, inertia measures the total intra-cluster variance.
        While minimizing inertia is the core objective of $K$-means, this value naturally decreases as $K$ increases, eventually reaching zero if every data point becomes its own individual cluster.
        Therefore, inertia is typically evaluated using the elbow method, where the optimal $K$ is identified at the inflection point where the rate of variance reduction sharply diminishes.
        
        \subsection{Silhouette index}
        To evaluate both cluster cohesion and cluster separation, the silhouette index (frequently referred to as the silhouette score or silhouette coefficient) is employed.
        Cluster cohesion describes how tightly grouped the points in a cluster are, while cluster separation describes how distinct the clusters are from one another.
        For a given data point $i$, the silhouette coefficient $s(i)$ is defined mathematically as:
        
        \begin{equation}
            s(i) = \frac{b(i) - a(i)}{\max(a(i), b(i))}
        \end{equation}
        
        where $a(i)$ is the mean distance between point $i$ and all other points assigned to the same cluster, and $b(i)$ is the minimum mean distance between point $i$ and all points in any other cluster, representing the nearest neighboring cluster \cite{wilbert_recency_2023}.
        
        The silhouette score ranges from $-1$ to $1$.
        A value close to $1$ indicates that the data point is perfectly matched to its own cluster and cleanly separated from neighboring clusters.
        A value near $0$ suggests the point is on or very close to the decision boundary between two clusters.
        Negative values indicate that the point may have been assigned to the wrong cluster.
        The global silhouette score of a model is calculated as the mean $s(i)$ across all data points, providing a straightforward and robust metric to mathematically determine the optimal partition count $K$.
        
    \section{Dimensionality reduction}
    \label{sec:dimensionality_reduction}
    
        \subsection{Principal component analysis}
        \label{subsec:pca}
        While partitioning algorithms like $K$-means group observations into distinct clusters, financial datasets are frequently characterized by high dimensionality.
        As the number of features $d$ increases, the concept of distance becomes inherently less discriminative, representing a mathematical phenomenon widely known as the curse of dimensionality.
        To mitigate this, Principal Component Analysis, or PCA, is often employed as a preliminary unsupervised learning step to reduce the feature space while preserving the maximum possible variance of the original dataset \cite{naeem_unsupervised_2023}.
        PCA seeks an orthogonal linear transformation that projects the data into a new $k$-dimensional subspace, where $k < d$, maximizing the variance of the projected data.
        The spatial relationships between the features are captured by the $d \times d$ sample covariance matrix.
        Solving the algebraic eigenvalue problem for the covariance matrix identifies eigenvectors, which dictate the direction of the principal axes, and eigenvalues, which quantify the magnitude of the dataset's variance explained along that specific axis.
        The first $k$ eigenvectors are concatenated to form a projection matrix, mapping the high-dimensional dataset into a reduced subspace.
        In the context of financial customer segmentation, applying PCA prior to clustering effectively filters out stochastic noise and redundant, collinear variables.
        This dimensionality reduction can accelerate optimization and stabilize cluster assignments by grounding distance calculations exclusively in the most statistically significant dimensions.
        
        \subsection{Uniform manifold approximation and projection}
        \label{subsec:umap}
        While PCA provides an efficient mechanism for capturing the global variance and linear relationships within a dataset, it inherently struggles to resolve complex, non-linear patterns.
        In high-dimensional financial datasets, data points frequently lie on complex manifolds that linear projections simply flatten.
        To address this limitation, non-linear dimensionality reduction techniques like Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection, or UMAP, are often considered \cite{mcinnes_umap_2020}.
        UMAP is a modern manifold learning technique grounded in Riemannian geometry and algebraic topology.
        Unlike PCA, UMAP operates by constructing a high-dimensional fuzzy topological representation of the data and subsequently optimizing a low-dimensional graph to be as structurally similar as possible.
        This optimization is achieved by minimizing the fuzzy set cross-entropy $C$ between the high-dimensional and low-dimensional graph weights.
        While theoretically robust for capturing non-linear topologies and maintaining local proximities of customer profiles, constructing the requisite high-dimensional fuzzy graphs is highly computationally intensive.
        For massive financial datasets containing millions of observations, UMAP often becomes computationally infeasible without aggressive down-sampling, limiting its utility as a primary reduction step in full-population pipelines.
        
    \section{Iterative descent optimization}
    \label{sec:descent_optimization}
    To understand the mechanics of large-scale clustering, it is necessary to first establish the general principles of iterative descent optimization.
    In applied machine learning, optimization problems frequently require minimizing a differentiable scalar cost function.
    When analytical solutions are computationally intractable, iterative methods such as Gradient Descent are employed to update parameters by taking steps in the direction of the negative gradient.
    Evaluating the full gradient at every iteration is computationally prohibitive for massive datasets where $n \gg 1$.
    Stochastic gradient methods bypass this bottleneck by approximating the true gradient using a small, randomly sampled subset of the data known as a mini-batch.
    Because the mini-batch gradient is an unbiased estimator of the true gradient, the algorithm descends the cost surface in expectation.
    To theoretically guarantee convergence in the presence of stochastic noise, the learning rate must strictly satisfy the Robbins-Monro conditions for stochastic approximation \cite{robbins_stochastic_1951}.
    
    \section{Data scaling and normalization}
    \label{sec:data_scaling}
    Distance-based clustering algorithms, such as $K$-means and $K$-medians, as well as variance-maximizing techniques like PCA, are mathematically sensitive to the scale of the input features.
    In credit card portfolios, financial variables inherently span vastly different magnitudes, for example, transaction counts ranging from 0 to 50 versus monetary spend spanning from \$0 to \$50,000.
    To ensure all variables contribute equally to the multidimensional topology, data scaling is a mandatory preprocessing step.
    
        \subsection{$Z$-score normalization}
        \label{subsec:zscore_scaling}
        A standard approach is $Z$-score normalization, also known as standard scaling, which rescales each feature column to have a mean of zero and a standard deviation of one \cite{pinheiro_impact_2025}. Mathematically, the transformed value $x_{\text{norm}}$ is calculated as:
        \begin{equation}
            x_{\text{norm}} = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}
            \label{eq:zscore}
        \end{equation}
        where $x$ is the original feature value, $\mu$ represents the mean of the feature column, and $\sigma$ denotes its standard deviation. While effective for roughly normally distributed data, it can still be skewed by extreme outliers.
                
        \subsection{Min-max scaling}
        \label{subsec:minmax_scaling}
        When the objective is to bound the data within a strict, predefined range, typically $[0, 1]$, min-max scaling is utilized. This transformation preserves the exact geometric shape of the original distribution but forces all features onto the same finite scale \cite{pinheiro_impact_2025}. The scaled value $x_{\text{scaled}}$ is given by:
        \begin{equation}
            x_{\text{scaled}} = \frac{x - x_{\text{min}}}{x_{\text{max}} - x_{\text{min}}}
            \label{eq:minmax}
        \end{equation}
        where $x_{\text{min}}$ and $x_{\text{max}}$ are the global minimum and maximum values of that feature column. Because it relies strictly on these global boundaries, it is highly vulnerable to anomalies. 
                
        \subsection{Robust scaling}
        \label{subsec:robust_scaling}
        Given that financial datasets are notoriously heavy-tailed, robust scaling often provides a more stable transformation \cite{pinheiro_impact_2025}. Instead of utilizing the mean and standard deviation, this method centers the data using the median, denoted as $Q_2$, and scales it according to the Interquartile Range (IQR). The robustly scaled value $x_{\text{robust}}$ is formulated as:
        \begin{equation}
            x_{\text{robust}} = \frac{x - Q_2}{Q_3 - Q_1}
            \label{eq:robust}
        \end{equation}
        where $Q_1$ and $Q_3$ represent the 25th and 75th percentiles of the feature distribution, respectively, such that $\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1$. By utilizing non-parametric statistics, the robust scaler ensures that the bulk of the customer data remains proportionate, preventing financial anomalies from distorting the geometry of the clustering space.
        
    \section{Feature engineering and selection}
    \label{sec:feature_engineering_selection}
    Preparing high-dimensional empirical datasets for unsupervised machine learning requires rigorous preprocessing.
    Observational data is frequently intrinsically noisy, highly skewed, and plagued by redundant variables.
    
        \subsection{Logarithmic transformation}
        \label{subsec:log_transform}
        A common characteristic of many continuous empirical features is a severe right-skewness.
        For distance-metric algorithms, these extreme right-tail outliers exert a disproportionate gravitational pull.
        To mitigate this, shifted logarithmic transformations, represented as $x' = \log(x + c)$, are frequently applied to compress the long tail and approximate a more normal distribution, mapping strict zero values exactly back to zero.
        
        \subsection{Near-zero variance filtering}
        \label{subsec:nzv_filtering}
        High-dimensional datasets frequently contain highly specialized or niche variables, resulting in a significantly sparse feature matrix.
        Features that exhibit Near-Zero Variance (NZV) contribute negligible discriminatory power to the clustering process \cite{ochoteco_asensio_predicting_2022}.
        To mathematically formalize this, let $x_j$ denote the $j$-th feature vector across $n$ observations.
        The sample variance for this feature is calculated as:
        \begin{equation}
            s_j^2 = \frac{1}{n-1} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_{ij} - \bar{x}_j)^2
        \end{equation}
        
        where $x_{ij}$ represents the value of the $j$-th feature for the $i$-th observation, and $\bar{x}_j$ is the sample mean of feature $j$.
        To address uninformative features, NZV filtering is employed as a primary feature selection step, algorithmically pruning features that fail to meet a predefined minimum variance threshold, $\tau_{nzv}$.
        Thus, any feature $j$ satisfying the condition $s_j^2 \le \tau_{nzv}$ is systematically removed from the feature space.
        
        \subsection{Correlation thresholding}
        \label{subsec:correlation_thresholding}
        Beyond sparsity, datasets frequently exhibit severe multicollinearity.
        In a Euclidean space, two perfectly correlated features will mathematically exert twice the weight on the distance calculation as a single, independent feature.
        To neutralize this structural bias, correlation thresholding is applied to prune redundant variables using the sample Pearson correlation coefficient, $\rho$.
        For any pair of features $x_j$ and $x_k$, the correlation is defined as:
        \begin{equation}
            \rho_{j,k} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_{ij} - \bar{x}_j)(x_{ik} - \bar{x}_k)}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_{ij} - \bar{x}_j)^2} \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_{ik} - \bar{x}_k)^2}}
        \end{equation}
        
        An absolute correlation threshold is established, which in this research is bounded at a strict magnitude of $\tau_{corr} = 0.90$.
        When a pair of features exhibits a linear relationship exceeding this limit, where $|\rho_{j,k}| > \tau_{corr}$, one feature from the pair is algorithmically removed.
        This process ensures an orthogonal, balanced feature space that prevents heavily correlated variables from dominating the subsequent clustering objective function \cite{mangal_comparative_2018}.
        
    \section{The label alignment problem in model comparison}
    \label{sec:hungarian}
    Evaluating and comparing multiple unsupervised learning algorithms presents a fundamental challenge.
    Each method independently assigns arbitrary identifiers to the clusters it discovers.
    For example, two completely different algorithms might successfully identify the exact same underlying grouping in the data, but one might label it "Cluster 0" while the other labels it "Cluster 3."
    To directly compare these clustering methods against each other, whether to measure their consensus or to evaluate them against a shared baseline, one must first solve the label alignment problem.
    This requires discovering an optimal mapping function that translates their disparate, arbitrary labels into a unified space \cite{sieranoja_clustering_2024}.
    Without establishing this alignment, computing the true discrepancy between the algorithms' outputs is mathematically impossible.
    Formally, to compare the structural agreement between the methods, the absolute discrepancy between their aligned sets is directly quantified.
    The Minimum Label Differences are defined as:
    \begin{equation}
        \text{Differences} = N - \sum_{i=1}^{N} \delta(g_i, \text{map}(a_i))
    \end{equation}
    where $N$ is the total number of data points, $g_i$ is the reference label derived from the baseline method or ground truth, $a_i$ is the predicted cluster label from the method being evaluated, $\delta$ is the Kronecker delta which yields $1$ if the labels match and $0$ otherwise, and $\text{map}(\cdot)$ is the optimal mapping function \cite{sieranoja_clustering_2024}.
    Because a dataset with $k$ clusters possesses $k!$ possible mapping permutations, finding the optimal $\text{map}(\cdot)$ is computationally framed as a linear assignment problem.
    By constructing a contingency matrix that quantifies the overlap between the methods' cluster assignments, the Kuhn-Munkres, or Hungarian, algorithm is utilized to deterministically isolate the 1-to-1 label pairing that minimizes the total label differences.
    This ensures that any remaining differences represent genuine structural disagreements between the clustering algorithms, rather than mere variations in their arbitrary naming conventions.

\chapter{Methodology}
\label{chap:Methodology}

This chapter outlines the data pipeline and machine learning workflow developed for this thesis. The structure of the chapter follows the sequential progression of the data pipeline: data acquisition, preprocessing, feature engineering, and unsupervised clustering. This specific sequence ensures that the dataset is systematically cleaned, filtered, and transformed before partitioning algorithms are applied, preventing raw database artifacts and noise from biasing the models.

    \section{Data acquisition and structure}
    \label{sec:data_acquisition}
    The dataset consists of annual-level data containing approximately one million observations across the years 2024 and 2025. Each observation represents a specific credit card agreement at a single cross-section in time, characterized by over 580 raw features. To maintain a consistent unit of analysis, individual records are tracked using a multi-index comprising the customer agreement ID and the data year of aggregation. 
    
    This baseline temporal and spatial data structure was selected based on two primary methodological rationales. First, utilizing an annual aggregation window helps smooth out temporary fluctuations. Because transactional data contains high short-term variance and noise driven by seasonal factors—such as holiday shopping periods or vacation spending—aggregating the raw transactional logs into annual snapshots isolates stable lifestyle habits and macro-level behavior rather than short-lived variations in spending velocity. 
    
    Second, treating the annual snapshots independently addresses the non-stationary nature of financial behavior. A customer's financial context, risk profile, and card utilization can change significantly from 2024 to 2025 due to shifts in income, employment, or credit needs. Bounding the data points strictly by year and treating them as independent observations allows the model to capture these distinct behavioral states accurately. This setup prevents historical habits from obscuring new behavioral trends, and it establishes the necessary foundation for the longitudinal transition analysis presented in the results.
    
    \section{Data preprocessing and heuristic exclusions}
    \label{sec:preprocessing}
    Before applying any machine learning algorithms, the raw dataset required several preprocessing steps to ensure data quality and relevance to the target customer population.
    
        \subsection{Data cleansing}
        The initial cleansing phase focused on standardizing the dataset and removing artifacts generated during the data export process.
        Analysis of the missing data revealed that rows containing null values, denoted as \texttt{NaN}, constituted less than 1\% of the total observations.
        Given this negligible proportion, listwise deletion was employed to drop these incomplete records.
        This approach was selected over imputation to prevent the introduction of synthetic bias, ensuring that all downstream distance calculations were based strictly on empirical observations.
        Furthermore, rows exhibiting fundamental structural errors, such as logically inconsistent temporal timestamps or corrupted feature mappings, were entirely removed from the analytical pipeline.
        
        \subsection{Rule-based segmentation}
        An optional exploratory step built into the pipeline is a rule-based segmentation approach.
        The rationale is to explicitly isolate specific, predefined customer cohorts prior to the application of unsupervised models.
        Two primary groups of interest were established for this pre-segmentation phase: Group Z and group C. These groups represent distinct behavioral or operational extremes within the portfolio based on specific internal business logic.
        By deterministically assigning these accounts to predefined segments beforehand, the primary clustering algorithms are freed from grouping obvious structural extremes, allowing them to focus entirely on parsing the nuanced variance within the active majority of the portfolio.
        
    \section{Feature engineering and transformation}
    \label{sec:feature_engineering}
    To manage dimensionality and ensure the models train on high-signal data, a standardized sequence of feature transformations and algorithmic filtration is applied to the dataset.

        \subsection{Logarithmic transformation}
        \label{subsec:log_transform}
        An experimental logarithmic transformation, \texttt{log1p}, is investigated as an optional preprocessing step to handle the extreme positive skewness typical of financial data. When enabled, this transformation is exclusively applied to aggregated transactional sum data, leaving other metric types such as counts, limits, and percentages in their original scale. This selective application is mathematically necessary due to the structural differences between feature types. Financial volumes span multiple orders of magnitude and are heavily right-skewed, meaning a small percentage of extreme accounts controls most of the variance. The logarithmic transformation compresses this long tail to prevent outliers from dominating distance calculations. Conversely, indicators such as transaction counts, risk limits, and utilization ratios are naturally bounded or structurally constrained. Applying a logarithmic transformation to these metrics would compress meaningful linear differences, making it harder for distance-based models to detect distinct variations in account behavior. Crucially, because this logarithmic transformation fundamentally alters the variance and the linear relationships between variables, its application directly impacts all subsequent filtration steps. Consequently, toggling this transformation dynamically changes the final feature space across all tested models.
        
        \subsection{Algorithmic filtration}
        \label{subsec:nzv_filtering_method}
        Following the transformation phase, automated data-agnostic filtration is applied to reduce computational bloat and eliminate redundant information. First, a near-zero variance filter with a strict threshold of $10^{-4}$ is implemented to discard non-informative, largely static features. Features that exhibit near-zero variance across the entire portfolio add flat coordinate baselines to the distance equations. Because they do not vary significantly between observations, they increase computational overhead without contributing discriminatory power to separate customer segments.
        
        Subsequently, a filter is applied to mitigate multicollinearity by dropping variables with a Pearson correlation coefficient exceeding an absolute magnitude of 0.90. In a distance-based metric space, if two features are highly collinear, such as total spend volume and average transaction size, that underlying behavioral dimension is effectively counted twice. This duplication artificially inflates the structural importance of that behavior during centroid optimization and skews the geometric boundaries of the resulting clusters. Applying correlation thresholding ensures an orthogonal, balanced feature space where each distinct behavioral proxy exerts an independent weight on the objective function.
                
    \section{Scaling and domain-specific weighting}
    \label{sec:scaling}
    Because distance-based clustering algorithms are highly sensitive to the magnitude of the input variables, rigorous scaling methodologies were required.
    
        \subsection{Normalization techniques}
        Before applying global scalers, manual pre-normalization is applied to naturally bounded features.
        For example, utilization rates are explicitly scaled by dividing by 100 to map them to a strict continuous range without distorting their original proportional meaning.
        Following this, three distinct global scaling techniques are evaluated across the pipeline: min-max, robust, and standard scaling.
        Testing multiple scalers ensures that the final clusters are driven by genuine behavioral patterns rather than artifacts introduced by forced normal distributions or outlier sensitivities.
        
        \subsection{Feature weighting}
        \label{subsec:FeatureWeigting}
        While explicit feature weighting was considered to balance the influence of distinct financial categories, it introduces significant complexity when implemented dynamically at scale.
        Consequently, targeted feature weighting is treated as an optional parameter, specifically utilized within the domain-driven experimental configurations representing the normal and improved cases.
        In these cases, five core financial domains were identified as holding equal theoretical importance: incoming payments, interest capitalization, credit limits, utilization rates, and financial or cash-like transactions.
        However, because the algorithmic filtration processes, such as the correlation and NZV filters, prune the number of aggregated sub-features unevenly across these categories, standard scaling would disproportionately favor domains that retained a higher volume of sub-features.
        To rectify this, a custom weighting function is applied immediately prior to dimensionality reduction.
        The total feature weight is distributed such that each of the five core domains contributes exactly 20\% of the aggregate weight.
        For a given domain $d$ with $n_d$ surviving features, each individual feature within that domain is multiplied by the factor $\frac{1}{5 \cdot n_d}$.
        This strict normalization ensures the unsupervised models evaluate each core financial behavior equally, regardless of its underlying dimensional footprint post-filtration.
        
    \section{Dimensionality reduction}
    \label{sec:dimensionality_reduction}
    Given the scale of the dataset, dimensionality reduction techniques were evaluated to compress the feature space and improve algorithmic efficiency prior to spatial clustering.
    
        \subsection{Principal component analysis}
        \label{subsec:pca}
        PCA is incorporated into the pipeline as an optional experimental variable rather than a default preprocessing step. The methodology systematically investigates and compares clustering outcomes both with and without PCA to objectively assess its necessity. When enabled, PCA projects the filtered features into a lower-dimensional orthogonal space while retaining a target cumulative variance, which helps mitigate the curse of dimensionality and accelerates optimization. However, this orthogonal transformation shifts the data into abstract principal components that represent linear combinations of the original variables, thereby obscuring direct feature interpretation. Because the final customer segments must eventually be delivered to non-technical business stakeholders who require explicit, deterministic rules for product campaigns and capital allocation, retaining direct linear interpretability is an operational priority. Treating PCA as an optional parameter allows the pipeline to empirically evaluate whether the mathematical improvements in spatial density and convergence speed justify the loss of direct behavioral explanation.
        
        \subsection{Alternative methods: manifold learning}
        \label{subsec:manifold}
        Alternative non-linear dimensionality reduction techniques were also considered during the development of the pipeline, specifically UMAP. UMAP is effective at capturing complex, non-linear patterns and preserving both local and global geometric relationships within datasets. However, preliminary testing revealed that constructing the high-dimensional fuzzy topological graphs required by UMAP was too computationally intensive for a dense matrix containing nearly one million annual observations, leading to processing bottlenecks. Utilizing UMAP within the primary workflow would have necessitated aggressive random down-sampling of the dataset. Because a primary objective of this thesis is to execute a full-population analysis on the active credit card base without data loss or sampling bias, UMAP was excluded from the final quantitative pipeline in favor of more scalable partitioning configurations.
        
    \section{Clustering algorithms}
    \label{sec:clustering_algorithms}
    The core of the machine learning workflow relies on distance-based, unsupervised partitioning algorithms to identify latent customer archetypes from the engineered feature space.
    
        \subsection{Model selection}
        \label{subsec:model_selection}
        The primary algorithms evaluated in this research are $K$-means and $K$-medians. Partitioning models were deliberately prioritized over complex, non-linear deep architectures because they establish a transparent baseline characterized by strong global consensus and linear explainability. In a retail banking environment, corporate stakeholders require deterministic rules to authorize capital allocation and product campaigns, making geometric transparency highly valuable. Furthermore, these partitioning frameworks provide an exceptionally scalable foundation for production environments. Because their mathematical structures rest on straightforward centroid or median shifts, they can easily adapt to modern large-scale extensions such as mini-batch optimizations and online streaming update rules. This algorithmic scalability ensures that the core architecture remains computationally viable and efficient even as the bank's underlying transaction volumes and portfolio sizes grow over time.
        
        \subsection{Hyperparameter tuning}
        \label{subsec:hyperparameter_tuning}
        Determining the optimal number of customer segments, denoted as $K$, requires tuning the models across a range of cluster counts against quantitative objective metrics. Inertia is evaluated to identify structural elbow points where the rate of intra-cluster variance reduction begins to sharply diminish. Concurrently, the silhouette score is used to measure and maximize global cluster cohesion and separation. However, calculating the exact pairwise silhouette coefficient over the entire dataset requires $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$ space and time complexity, which is computationally intractable for approximately one million observations. To mitigate this computational bottleneck without sacrificing statistical validity, a stratified random sample of 50,000 observations was leveraged as an evaluation space. This stratified sampling approach preserves the original cluster proportions and density distributions of the full population, providing a representative and computationally viable framework to mathematically determine the optimal partition count.
        
    \section{Cluster profiling and interpretation}
    \label{sec:cluster_profiling}
    Following algorithmic partitioning, the resulting mathematical clusters must be translated back into actionable financial insights to ensure they are useful for strategic decision-making.
    
        \subsection{Evaluation in original feature space}
        \label{subsec:original_space_evaluation}
        Because distance-based algorithms require scaled and normalized data to optimize their objective functions without dimensional bias, the raw cluster outputs exist in an abstract vector space that lacks immediate business context. To resolve this limitation, all resulting cluster assignments are mapped directly back to the original, unscaled dataset. This mapping is methodologically necessary because normalized values obscure the actual physical units of banking operations. Evaluating the customer archetypes in the original feature space allows analysts and business managers to interpret the performance of each cluster using concrete, real-world metrics, such as actual monetary transaction sums, genuine utilization percentages, and exact transaction count profiles. 
        
        \subsection{Visualization and temporal dynamics}
        \label{subsec:temporal_dynamics_method}
        To profile the segments, comparative statistical visualizations, including customized boxplots and median-centric comparative analyses, are utilized to isolate defining financial characteristics across groups. Finally, the methodology concludes by examining temporal dynamics to examine customer segment transitions over time. Credit card behavior is inherently fluid, and analyzing a snapshot from a single year can obscure long-term trends or stability. To capture these shifting patterns, the analysis tracks accounts possessing multiple annual observations across the delimited 2024 to 2025 timeframe using a cross-tabulation of the initial and subsequent cluster assignments.
        
        The raw migration counts within this cross-tabulation are row-normalized to construct an empirical transition probability matrix. Row-normalization is mathematically required because the baseline sizes of the clusters vary significantly. By converting raw transaction counts into uniform proportions, the matrix provides a standardized measure of behavioral stickiness across all customer segments. This approach allows the model to shift from a static partitioning tool to a dynamic lifecycle framework, enabling the bank to quantify the exact probability that an account will either remain stable within its current behavioral tier or migrate to a new operational archetype in the following fiscal year.
        
    \section{Experimental design: Pipeline configurations}
    \label{sec:experimental_design}
    Having established the technical pipeline, the methodology was deployed across three distinct experimental cases to rigorously evaluate the impact of feature selection and data preprocessing on the resulting customer archetypes.
    
        \subsection{Case 1: The naive baseline}
        The naive case simulates a purely algorithmic approach, relying on automated dimensionality reduction rather than financial domain knowledge.
        An initial, highly redundant feature space of 408 continuous variables was generated using exhaustive temporal aggregations across all available base metrics.
        Applying the standardized algorithmic filtration, comprising NZV and correlation limits, left the naive model with an uncurated space of 220 features, or 157 features if logarithmically transformed.
        As noted in Section~\ref{sec:feature_engineering}, this final feature count fluctuates depending on whether the optional logarithmic transformation is applied, as it alters which variables survive filtration.
        Crucially, in this baseline case, the massive sub-population of completely inactive accounts is retained, challenging the algorithms to handle the zero-heavy distributions natively.
        
        \subsection{Case 2: The normal case}
        Recognizing the potential noise of a purely data-agnostic approach, the methodology was pivoted toward business-logic-driven feature selection.
        The feature space was drastically scaled down by isolating core financial behaviors, specifically utilization rates and specific incoming payment or transaction categories.
        Applying standard aggregations exclusively to these metrics reduced the initial dataset to 29 features.
        A secondary application of the NZV and correlation filters further refined this space down to roughly 19 distinct variables, or 16 if logarithmically transformed.
        Like the naive baseline, the normal case implemented no heuristic pre-clustering.
        
        \subsection{Case 3: The improved case}
        A significant structural challenge within the raw financial data is the substantial presence of inactive accounts, which act as a mathematical sink and cause clustering algorithms to become trapped in suboptimal local minima. It utilizes the same tightly curated, domain-driven feature space established in the normal case.
        However, it activates the heuristic pre-segmentation step, detailed in Section~\ref{sec:preprocessing}, to deterministically filter out inactive accounts before applying the unsupervised models.
        Inactive accounts were explicitly defined and isolated as profiles exhibiting exactly zero user-initiated transactions.
        By partitioning these accounts beforehand, the subsequent models are forced to focus entirely on the variance within active financial behaviors.

\chapter{Discussion}
\label{chap:Discussion}

This chapter synthesizes the empirical findings detailed in Chapter~\ref{chap:Results}, bridging mathematical cluster optimization with practical financial strategy.
By analyzing algorithmic behaviors against the realities of a retail banking portfolio, this section explores the structural necessity of the pipeline's preprocessing choices, the trade-offs in dimensionality reduction, and the economic implications of the resulting customer archetypes.
\section{The structural necessity of preprocessing constraints}

The evaluation of scaling methodologies confirmed that the intrinsic geometry of financial data requires rigid spatial boundaries.
While both min-max and standard scaling produced viable cluster distributions, min-max scaling was deliberately selected as the definitive global scaler due to its specific handling of absolute limits and domain characteristics.
In credit card portfolios, many core features inherently share base units, such as absolute monetary values or frequency metrics.
A defining characteristic of this data is its strict lower bound. An account cannot have negative transactions or negative spend.
min-max scaling explicitly preserves this physical reality. By binding the data to a strict $[0, 1]$ interval, an original value of zero maps perfectly to a scaled value of zero.
Conversely, standard scaling normalize features by centering them around a mean of zero.
Applying this to heavily right-skewed financial data artificially shifts genuine zero-activity metrics into negative vector space.
This shift mathematically destroys the data's natural sparsity and distorts the proportional distance between a dormant behavior and an active one.
Because the features share similar physical interpretations, bounding them to a proportional $[0, 1]$ span ensures they are evaluated on an equal playing field without violating their natural lower limits.
The empirical results support this theoretical preference; min-max scaling achieved a higher and more stable peak silhouette score compared to standard scaling, confirming its superior preservation of cluster cohesion.
Finally, while theoretical arguments can be made for robust scaling in heavy-tailed data, its application to this specific domain resulted in severely skewed $L_2$ norm distances.
As shown in Table~\ref{tab:scaler_comparison_157_features}, it allowed unbounded transaction sums to disproportionately dominate the spatial topography, collapsing the portfolio into a near-degenerate single cluster.
Similarly, the profound divergence between the uncurated naive baseline and the domain-driven normal approach highlights the curse of dimensionality in unsupervised learning.
The bloated, 157-dimensional naive space mathematically diluted the distance metrics.
By utilizing rigorous business-logic curation to reduce the feature space to 16 variables, the distance-based algorithms were able to evaluate core behaviors without being blinded by multicollinear noise.

\section{Addressing operational extremes via pre-segmentation}

A fundamental vulnerability exposed during early pipeline testing was the algorithms' sensitivity to structural sparsity.
Group Z alone accounts for 33.79\% of the data, representing accounts with exactly zero user-initiated transactions, as detailed in Table~\ref{tab:pipeline_summary}.
When included in the primary dataset, this demographic acted as a massive mathematical sink in Euclidean space.
As visualized in the left panel of the baseline boxplots in Figure~\ref{fig:presegmentation_boxplots}, the inclusion of dormant accounts heavily crushed the internal variance toward zero.
This forced the algorithm into generating a trivial, high-density binary boundary between active and inactive accounts.
By deterministically filtering these known operational extremes, the variance within the core active portfolio expanded.
Forcing the algorithm to evaluate only the 633,279 active profiles compelled the model to learn genuine financial behaviors rather than simplistic density divides.

\section{Dimensionality reduction and interpretability trade-offs}

While PCA successfully stabilized the uncurated naive case, detailed in Table~\ref{tab:pca_naive_comparison}, acting as an algorithmic safeguard against poor data quality, its application within the optimized pipeline presented a significant business trade-off.
In the final case, applying PCA yielded only marginal spatial density improvements while reducing computational overhead, as demonstrated in Table~\ref{tab:pca_comparison} and Figure~\ref{fig:pca_silhouette_comparison}.
However, the resulting principal components obfuscated direct feature interpretation. Because the practical utility of these clusters relies on business stakeholders understanding and acting upon explicit behavioral traits, retaining direct linear interpretability was prioritized over the slight mathematical gains offered by orthogonal compression.
Furthermore, applying explicit domain weights proved vital for breaking the dataset's tendency toward binary separation.
While the introduction of weights slightly lowered the global silhouette score, which is a recognized mathematical tradeoff when manipulating feature variance, it successfully fractured the portfolio into a richer, actionable $K=4$ structure as contrasted in Table~\ref{tab:weighting_comparison} and Figure~\ref{fig:weighting_silhouette}.

\section{The computational cost of robustness: evaluating $K$-medians}

The comparative evaluation of $K$-means and $K$-medians effectively illustrated how different objective functions navigate financial noise.
The $K$-medians algorithm, optimizing the $L_1$ norm or Manhattan distance, demonstrated theoretical robustness against structural outliers.
It actively bypassed the dense binary traps that ensnared $K$-means when processing uncurated data, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:naive_kmeans_vs_kmedians_metrics}.
However, this robustness incurs a severe computational penalty that makes $K$-medians largely unsuitable for naive, high-dimensional pipelines.
As detailed in Table~\ref{tab:kmeans_vs_kmedians_comparison}, executing $K$-medians on the uncurated 220-feature space required 65.73 seconds to converge, nearly seven times longer than the 9.64 seconds required by standard $K$-means.
While $K$-medians successfully identified a more nuanced $K=3$ structure, the extensive runtime heavily hinders its viability for automated iterations on massive, uncurated feature sets.
Furthermore, once applied to the fully optimized, anomaly-free matrix, the $L_2$ optimization of $K$-means generated substantially denser, more compact substructures at a fraction of the time, cementing its selection for the final pipeline as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:kmeans_vs_kmedians_metrics} and Table~\ref{tab:kmeans_vs_kmedians_comparison}.

\section{The viability of mini-batch $K$-means at scale}

To address the computational bottlenecks associated with high-dimensional data, mini-batch $K$-means was evaluated as a scalable alternative.
The experimental results, shown in Table~\ref{tab:kmeans_vs_mbkmeans_comparison}, proved its viability as a rapid exploratory tool on massive, uncurated datasets.
When applied to the 220-feature naive pipeline, mini-batch reduced the convergence time by more than half, dropping from 9.64 seconds to 4.54 seconds.
Crucially, this speed increase did not come at the cost of analytical depth;
instead, it exposed a structural vulnerability in the standard baseline.
The standard $K$-means algorithm optimized via full-batch Lloyd updates became trapped in a dense, non-convex local minimum on the 220-feature uncurated matrix, forcing a primitive binary partition ($K=2$).
Conversely, the mini-batch implementation updates centroids stochastically based on random subsets.
This injection of stochastic noise effectively acted as a regularizer, enabling the algorithm to bypass the initialization traps of the uncurated space and resolve the latent $K=4$ structure.
This demonstrates that while domain curation reduces the need for stochastic approximation, mini-batch $K$-means offers intrinsic algorithmic resilience when navigating unrefined, high-dimensional data landscapes.
However, the results also inverted its expected utility once the dataset was properly curated.
While highly effective in massive dimensional spaces, it introduced unnecessary stochastic instability when applied to the refined 19-variable space, as observed in Figure~\ref{fig:mbkmeans_vs_kmeans_metrics}.
Because the domain-driven feature engineering solved the dimensionality problem at its root, standard $K$-means operated near-instantaneously on the final matrix, rendering stochastic batch estimation functionally obsolete for this specific optimized architecture.

\section{Economic profiling and behavioral stickiness}

The translation of abstract spatial boundaries into unscaled financial features validates the pipeline's utility, as depicted in Figures~\ref{fig:archetype_boxplots} and~\ref{fig:kmeans_0_detailed}.
The four isolated archetypes, detailed in Table~\ref{tab:final_cluster_distribution}, offer a sophisticated taxonomy of the active portfolio:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{KMEANS\_0:} Comprising 13.56\% of the total portfolio, this segment captures customers with average baseline activity in Feature\_1, but distinguishes them through high secondary utilization in Feature\_2 and a unique above-average signature in Feature\_3.
    \item \textbf{KMEANS\_1:} Comprising 26.25\% of the total portfolio, this segment represents the largest portion of the active base.
    Exhibiting lower overall activity, this group acts as a transitional bridge between true dormancy in group Z and higher engagement tiers.
    \item \textbf{KMEANS\_2 and KMEANS\_3:} Both groups capture the upper limits of prime utilization in Feature\_1, but their geometric separation is driven by distinct divergences in subsequent financial habits, specifically across Feature\_2 and Feature\_4.
\end{itemize}

Finally, the temporal transition matrix in Figure~\ref{fig:kmeans_transition} confirms that while customer behavior is inherently non-stationary, the defined archetypes are highly structured.
With retention rates generally exceeding 50\% year-over-year, the clusters demonstrate strong longitudinal stability.
Furthermore, quantifying specific migration vectors, such as the 10\% probability of group Z accounts activating into KMEANS\_1, provides a rigorous empirical foundation for targeted, lifecycle-driven portfolio management.

\chapter{Potential improvements and future work}
\label{chap:Improvements}

While this thesis successfully demonstrates a scalable, end-to-end pipeline for algorithmic customer segmentation, the methodology inherently involves mathematical and operational trade-offs.
Building upon the foundational baseline established in this research, several avenues for future work can further refine the clustering architecture and capture deeper, more complex nuances in customer behavior.
\section{Compositional feature engineering and proportional normalization}
The empirical success of the min-max scaling, which effectively bounded the feature space to a strict $[0, 1]$ interval, suggests that the clustering algorithms perform optimally when evaluating normalized, proportional geometries.
Currently, the methodology relies on absolute aggregated sums for each financial category.
While explicit domain weighting helped balance these categories, utilizing absolute monetary values means the algorithm inherently evaluates both the behavioral preference of where a customer spends, and the magnitude of how much wealth they possess simultaneously.
This can sometimes cause $K$-means to disproportionately group accounts based on sheer volume rather than nuanced transaction habits.
A highly promising avenue for future work is the transition from absolute aggregations to compositional data normalization.
Instead of feeding the model the absolute sum of each category, future iterations should normalize these values as a percentage of the customer's total activity, for example, defining a specific transaction category as representing 40\% of the account's total transaction volume.
To ensure the overall scale of the customer's financial footprint is not lost, the model would retain a single, isolated feature representing Total Expenditure.
By mathematically decoupling the scale of spending from the composition of spending, distance-based algorithms could more effectively isolate true behavioral archetypes, rather than clustering primarily on wealth boundaries.

\section{Temporal clustering and time-series analysis}
The current methodology relies on aggregating raw transactional records into unified annual snapshots.
While this approach successfully stabilizes short-term noise to isolate macro-level customer archetypes, it inherently collapses the temporal dimension.
As a result, intra-year seasonality, short-term behavioral shifts, and sequence-dependent spending habits are obscured.
Future research should move away from strict static aggregation and explore temporal clustering algorithms.
Utilizing techniques such as Dynamic Time Warping, or recurrent neural network-based autoencoders would allow the models to cluster the actual temporal trajectories of the accounts.
Evaluating chronological transaction sequences rather than static sums would provide deeper insights into the velocity of customer lifecycle changes.

\section{Hierarchical and second-stage sub-clustering}
The optimized $K$-means pipeline effectively established a robust macro-level taxonomy comprising four distinct active archetypes.
However, evaluating the final clusters in their original feature space reveals a degree of structural incompleteness.
Certain features continue to exhibit heavily skewed distributions with significant outliers, leading to overlapping behavioral boundaries across the defined segments.
To address this variance, future iterations should explore a hierarchical or second-stage clustering framework.
By isolating a specific macro-cluster, such as the highly populated transitional group KMEANS\_1, and applying a secondary, localized clustering algorithm directly to that subset, analysts could extract highly granular micro-behaviors that are otherwise overshadowed by the global variance of the full portfolio.

\section{Non-linear dimensionality reduction and soft clustering}
To prioritize explicit business interpretability, linear efficiency, and computational scale, this study primarily relied on PCA and algorithms optimizing the $L_2$ norm.
While this linear approach is highly effective as an initial, explainable clustering strategy, high-dimensional financial data frequently lies on complex, non-linear manifolds.
Future methodologies should investigate the utilization of UMAP.
Although running UMAP on the full population was computationally prohibitive for this thesis, applying partial UMAP to strategically down-sampled subsets or specific pre-segmented cohorts could unlock complex non-linear relationships.
Furthermore, these non-linear projections could be paired with density-based or probabilistic clustering algorithms, such as Hierarchical Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise (HDBSCAN) or Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM).
Specifically, GMM would be highly beneficial for addressing the cluster overlap observed in the current results.
Because GMM utilize a soft clustering approach, they assign a mathematical probability of an account belonging to multiple clusters rather than forcing a strict, deterministic boundary.
This probabilistic framework accurately mirrors the fluid, overlapping realities of retail banking behavior and would serve as a powerful evolution of the methodology presented in this research.

\section{Alternative algorithmic engines and initialization protocols}
The unsupervised architecture developed in this thesis relied exclusively on standard Lloyd's algorithm for full-batch partitioning and stochastic gradient descent for mini-batch refinements.
This configuration provided a robust baseline with mathematically sound convergence.
However, standard Lloyd's algorithm scales poorly on massive financial datasets due to its brute-force assignment mechanism.
It computes pairwise distances between every single observation and every cluster centroid during each iteration.
This layout creates an immense computational bottleneck.

To expand throughput as portfolio volumes grow, future work must bypass these distance calculations entirely.
Integrating Elkan's algorithm into the pipeline is a highly promising avenue.
By exploiting the triangle inequality, it establishes rigid upper and lower geometric bounds between data points and cluster centers.
This relationship allows the system to algorithmically skip redundant calculations without altering the final cluster topology.
Concurrently, online optimization engines warrant deep exploration. MacQueen’s $K$-means algorithm offers unique benefits here. It stream-processes observations one by one.
Centroids update instantly. This design allows the framework to scale linearly.
Alternatively, fast Markov chain Monte Carlo approximation frameworks like the AFK-MC2 seeding algorithm could be deployed.
Quantifying the precise interplay between these advanced seeding routines and accelerated distance engines will reveal if complex, latent customer segments can be unmasked with even greater computational efficiency.

\chapter{Conclusion}
\label{chap:Conclusion}

The modern retail banking landscape requires robust, scalable methodologies to distill vast, high-dimensional transactional data into actionable insights.
This thesis successfully designed, evaluated, and implemented an end-to-end unsupervised machine learning pipeline.
It cleanly translates complex credit card portfolio data into distinct, economically meaningful customer archetypes.
Algorithmic segmentation cannot rely on raw computational power alone. This reality was demonstrated by systematically deploying our architecture across three configurations: the data-agnostic naive Baseline, the domain-curated normal case, and the optimized improved case.
Analytical success requires a highly curated hybrid framework. The empirical findings establish that deterministic heuristic pre-segmentation is structurally vital.
Group Z represents a massive dormant sub-population containing 33.79\% of the total portfolio.
Isolating these operational extremes prevents the primary partitioning models from collapsing into trivial density divides.
The algorithms can then optimize exclusively for genuine behavioral variance within the active majority.
Furthermore, this study highlights the mathematical necessity of domain-driven feature engineering and rigid spatial boundaries.
These options are far superior to data-agnostic dimensionality reduction. Bounding our feature representations via min-max scaling effectively preserved the intrinsic, zero-bounded proportional geometries of financial variables.
Conversely, robust scaling led to near-degenerate partitions. Concurrently, implementing our custom domain-weighting function proved critical.
It fractured primitive, binary spatial groupings into a richer, actionable four-cluster structure where $K=4$.
Under these optimized conditions, standard $K$-means clustering emerged as the most efficient and robust partition engine.
It generates cohesive sub-populations while operating near-instantaneously compared to the high computational overhead of $K$-medians.
Ultimately, this thesis bridges the gap between theoretical cluster optimization and practical financial strategy.
The resulting customer archetypes move beyond simple, manual heuristics to capture latent, multivariate transactional habits.
Supported by empirical evidence of year-over-year longitudinal stability exceeding 50\% retention, this methodology provides Avarda Bank AB with a reliable, data-driven foundation for lifecycle portfolio management, targeted customer engagement, and future advancements in dynamic behavioral modeling.
